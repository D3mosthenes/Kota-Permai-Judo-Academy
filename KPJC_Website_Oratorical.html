Why Judo

The oldest form of
complete education.

In 1877 a seventeen year old university student named Jigoro Kano enrolled in his first jujutsu class. He covered his bruised body after every session with a foul smelling homemade salve and earned among his classmates the permanent name Kano the Odiferous. And he could not beat a training partner named Kanekichi Fukushima. Fukushima was large and powerful and needed no particular technique because his size was technique enough. He simply needed to be himself and that was sufficient.

And Kano did not respond to this by training harder in the conventional sense. He had craftsmen build wooden dolls he could bend into postures, working out the geometry of how a small body might move a large one. He went to the National Library and studied anatomy manuals. He read European wrestling manuals alongside ancient Japanese scrolls. What he was looking for was the governing law beneath the technique, the principle that, once understood, renders the question of size irrelevant. He found it. And then he built a system around it that is still standing.

What Kano founded in 1882 was not a sport. He was uncomfortable with that word his entire life. He was an educator who became the Father of Physical Education in Japan and judo was the vehicle by which the body and the character were educated simultaneously, because he believed and proved with decades of evidence that they cannot in fact be educated separately.

There is a woman named Noriko Yasuda who came to Kano's dojo in 1884. She was weak and sickly, mentally as well as physically. Kano had her eat well and do light exercises and then gradually enter the mat. And a year into the training she accompanied Kano and a mixed group on a climb to the summit of Mount Fuji. She reached the top first.

There is a man named Kyuzo Mifune who stood barely five feet tall and was grateful to his parents for giving him a small body because it forced him to train twice as hard as everyone else. His motto: "If my opponents train one hour I will train two. If they train two I will train three." He was never defeated in formal competition. He was still teaching on the mat at seventy three.

There is a woman named Katsuko Kosaki who found a book about judo in a bookstore at eighteen, read it cover to cover, and decided then and there that judo was for her. She trained in secret from her parents. She defeated three male opponents in a row at the rank examination. In 1933 she became the first woman black belt in the history of the Kodokan. She opened her own dojo two years after that.

These are not exceptional people who happened to find judo. These are ordinary people who were made extraordinary by it. That is the proposition. And the evidence for it is a hundred and forty three years old and still accumulating.

Kota Permai Judo Club · The Way of Judo · A Cosmology

The Cosmology
of Judo.

The principles that Jigoro Kano built into the Kodokan in 1882 were not techniques. They were a way of understanding the world and the person one becomes by submitting to its discipline honestly and for long enough that the body forgets to resist.

I
The Origin · Tokyo, 1877

The Boy Who Could Not
Simply Fight.

In 1877 a seventeen year old university student named Jigoro Kano enrolled in his first jujutsu class at the clinic and dojo of Hachinosuke Fukuda. He was small. He covered his bruised body every evening with a foul smelling homemade salve and earned among his classmates the permanent name Kano the Odiferous. And there was a training partner named Kanekichi Fukushima who defeated him every single time they trained together, not through technique but through size, and that was all he needed.

Another man at this impasse would have simply trained harder. Kano did something different entirely. He asked a sumo wrestler for advice and found sumo unhelpful. He had a craftsman build wooden dolls he could bend into different shapes, working out by hand and by eye the geometry of how a small body might move a large one. He went to the National Library in Ueno and studied anatomy manuals. He read European wrestling manuals, including what was likely a Dutch manual from the seventeenth century, alongside ancient Japanese ryu scrolls. From all of that he devised a throw called kata guruma, the shoulder wheel throw, and the first time he tried it on Fukushima it worked. Not because he had found a trick. Because he had found a principle.

The principle was this. A smaller body could defeat a larger one not by matching its force but by understanding where that force goes and where it does not, by yielding at the exact moment resistance would cost the most, and by redirecting what the larger body gives into the very throw the larger body has, without ever realizing it, already begun. Kano did not invent this idea. It had been present in the jujutsu schools of Japan for centuries. What he had done was arrive at it independently and rationally and empirically, not through transmission from a master and not through mystical experience but through the systematic investigation of anatomy and leverage and the actual mechanics of two human bodies in motion.

This is the founding temperament of Kodokan judo. Not the warrior tradition of inherited schools and not the mysticism of energy cultivation but the rational and relentless investigation of how the body actually works, conducted by a man who would not accept "because that is how it has always been done" as an answer to any question about anything. This temperament produced a system that is still being practiced in a hundred and forty three countries. It began with a seventeen year old and some wooden dolls.

"From Master Fukuda, I learned what my life's work would be. From Master Masamoto, I learned the subtle nature of kata. From Master Iikubo, I learned varied techniques and the importance of timing." Jigoro Kano · In his memoirs · On his three teachers before the founding
II
The Founding · Eisho ji Temple, Tokyo · June 5th 1882

A Twelve Mat Room
at a Buddhist Temple.

On the fifth of June in 1882 Jigoro Kano was twenty two years old. He had rented rooms at Eisho ji, a small Jodo sect Buddhist temple in Tokyo. The largest room was twelve mats in size and it doubled as a dojo. There were no set practice times. If someone showed up to train in the morning or during the day or late at night then the furniture was moved aside and whoever was there trained together. Only nine students formally registered in 1884. Eight the year after. Ten the year after that. Twenty seven fully committed students in the first three years of the Kodokan's existence. The training was so severe it began to damage the temple itself. Floorboards shattered. Memorial tablets tumbled from the altar. Roof tiles were knocked loose. The head priest eventually demanded Kano move the dojo, and so a small makeshift twelve mat space was built by one of the temple gates. Kano hung a sign on the building. Kodokan. Institute for Study of the Way.

"Kano Sensei trained just as hard if not harder than we did. It was judo, judo, judo. We drank mugi tea with a bit of sugar and ate rice seasoned only with lotus roots from the temple pond. Mostly I remember being sleepy and hungry but I never once thought of running away." Tsunejiro Tomita · The Kodokan's first student · In his journal

What distinguishes this founding from every other martial arts school established in Japan that century is not its size, which was negligible, but the absolute clarity of its purpose. Kano was not preserving a school. He was building a system. He compared jujutsu to Hinayana Buddhism, a small vehicle with a limited vision. Kodokan judo would be the Mahayana. A big vehicle that embraced both the individual and society as a whole. "If the work of a human being does not benefit society," he declared at the founding, "that person's existence is in vain." This is not a martial arts principle. It is a civilizational one.

And then Kano left behind a calligraphic motto he returned to again and again throughout his life, brushing it at lectures and at ceremonies and on pieces of paper given away to students who asked: Suiteki Sekisen. Drops of water wear down a stone. Not force. Persistence. The patient application of small and consistent effort over a timescale that most people do not have the courage to commit to. And then another motto, this one for the parents in the room: Shosofudoryoku. No effort in youth, no gain when old. Not as an admonishment. As a description of how the body actually works.

III
The Breakthrough · 1883

If My Partner Pulls,
I Push.

Kano's teacher Iikubo continued to visit the dojo after the founding, sauntering in samurai style and carrying a heavy iron fan in his sash in case ruffians attacked him on the way. He dominated Kano as usual. And then one day in 1883 something shifted. Kano grasped in the fullness of his body rather than only in his mind what he had known as theory for years. If my partner pulls, I push. If he pushes, I pull. He threw Iikubo three times in succession. Iikubo bestowed a formal teaching license on Kano that autumn and gave him the transmission scrolls of the school.

And Kano was careful, in describing this moment to his students later, to attribute it not to mystical experience as martial artists so often do but to years of careful investigation and a rational approach to the art and tireless experimentation with his own trainees. The breakthrough was not sudden. It was the arrival of an understanding that had been accumulating since the wooden dolls and the anatomy manuals six years before. It felt sudden only because it revealed itself all at once. This is also how all genuine learning works. The body deposits experience in increments too small to feel and then one day you throw your teacher three times in a row.

There is a small story Kano told later in life about a mongoose he watched in Okinawa in 1927, in one of those exhibitions they staged for tourists where a mongoose and a poisonous adder are made to fight. He said the mongoose killed the adder because it practiced judo. It dodged the adder's strikes and immediately counterattacked with perfect timing. He was not joking. He was entirely serious and entirely right.

This is the cosmological core of judo. Not the specific technique but the governing principle the technique expresses. Force meeting force is the least efficient possible use of energy. The body that yields at the right moment and redirects what is given to it is doing something more intelligent, not less effective, than the body that simply resists. And this principle does not apply only to judo. This is what Kano understood and what distinguishes him from every other martial arts systematizer of his era. He was not describing how to throw people. He was describing how to move through a world that is always pushing and pulling, always presenting force that can either be resisted at great cost or redirected at very little.

He called this understanding judo. He meant it to be practiced on the mat first and in every other domain of life immediately after. The child who trains ukemi on a Saturday morning in Kota Kemuning is not just learning a judo technique. She is depositing experience into the body's memory and the body's memory is the most durable kind there is. Years later in a context that has nothing to do with judo something in that deposited experience will surface and the people around her will notice it and will not be able to name what it is.

Jigoro Kano · Asked how a judo man would handle himself against a bear

"Bears are ferocious when cornered but they will shy away from loud noises or bright lights. The best kind of judo is to keep the bear at bay with proper precautions rather than attempt to confront it face to face."

This is also, it is worth noting, excellent life advice.
IV
The Two Principles · 精力善用 · 自他共栄

The Philosophy
Made Explicit.

Kano eventually codified the governing philosophy of Kodokan judo in two principles. They are not techniques and they are not rules. They are a description of how the world works when a person pays close enough attention to it and what becomes available to a person who has internalized them so deeply that they act on them without deliberation. They are what a good education produces. And judo is in Kano's conception precisely that, a good education applied to the body first so that the mind and the character follow in their own time and in their own way.

精力善用 Seiryoku Zenyo
Maximum Efficiency, Minimum Effort

All available energy, physical and mental and moral alike, should be applied with maximum efficiency toward whatever end is being pursued. Waste nothing. Resist nothing that need not be resisted. Apply force precisely where it is needed at the precise moment it is needed and not a moment before or after. This is the first lesson of the mat and it becomes, through years of practice, the founding habit of every domain the trained person enters afterward. A child who has spent years learning this through the body has learned it in the most durable way that exists.

自他共栄 Jita Kyoei
Mutual Welfare and Benefit

No individual improvement is complete unless it contributes to the improvement of others. The opponent on the mat is not an obstacle. The opponent is the teacher. You cannot improve without a partner and your partner cannot improve without you. Kano believed that this principle, absorbed on the mat and carried off it, is the foundation of good citizenship and of a good society. Judo is not a preparation for life. It is a practice of life, with the volume turned up high enough that you cannot miss the lesson even if you are not looking for it.

Kyuzo Mifune, the greatest technical judoka of the generation after Kano and a man who was never defeated in formal competition, composed five principal points of judo practice that describe these two principles in the language of someone who had lived on the mat for decades. And the last of his five points is this: do not place hope in finding a secret technique. Polish the mind through ceaseless training. That is the key to effective technique. Kano had been saying the same thing since 1882 in the language of a scholar and an educator and here is Mifune saying it in the language of a man who has thrown everyone he has ever met. The message is identical. The mat and the library are the same room.

V
Ki and Timing · The Kito Ryu Transmission · 1883

The Intelligence
of Yielding.

The third of Kano's teachers, Tsunetoshi Iikubo of the Kito ryu, trained him in a system philosophically distinct from the earlier schools. Where the Tenjin Shin'yo ryu was practical and technique heavy, the Kito ryu was philosophical and timing centred. Its foundational texts, the Kito ryu Densho Chusaku, describe the relationship between ki and yin and yang and perfect timing in language that Kano absorbed and later rationalized into seiryoku zenyo and the modern science of leverage and center of gravity.

These are not two different things that Kano was navigating between. The ancient philosophical language of ki and the modern rational language of efficiency are, for anyone who has genuinely practiced judo long enough, two descriptions of the same phenomenon. The moment when a throw works perfectly does not feel like mechanics. It feels like something else entirely. And both descriptions are true at the same time because the body knows things that the vocabulary of either age struggles to fully capture.

Kano received this text in the same year he threw Iikubo for the first time. He did not need to choose between the mystical language and the rational language. He absorbed the principle and expressed it in the language his era could use and trusted that anyone who practiced long enough would eventually know what both descriptions were pointing toward.

From the Kito Ryu Densho Chusaku · 17th Century · Transmitted to Jigoro Kano by Tsunetoshi Iikubo, 1883

"Ki fulfills the body. When ki arises it is called yang; when it is suppressed it is called yin. If the body is not set aright ki cannot be brought forth. The secret teaching of our system is this: continually polish your ki, do not be captivated by worldly objects, and keep firmly to the fundamentals. When the opponent attacks with yin use yang to win. When the opponent attacks with yang use yin to emerge victorious. If you abandon force and utilize ki the opponent will defeat himself. This is the key to total victory. If your heart is correct your technique will be correct."

Kito Ryu Densho Chusaku

A parent reading this passage may feel, if they are honest, that it does not sound like a description of sport. It does not. It sounds like a description of how to raise a child. Do not be captivated by worldly objects. Keep firmly to the fundamentals. If your heart is correct your technique will be correct. Kano thought so too. The mat and the moral life were for him the same classroom and he never tired of saying so.

VI
"If you want to understand a teacher, look at his students."

What the Kodokan
Produced.

Kano wrote: if you want to understand a teacher, look at his students. And so let us look. The students of the Kodokan went on to careers that no martial arts curriculum could have predicted because the curriculum was never only martial arts. It was an education in how to apply maximum efficiency and mutual benefit to any domain one entered. The mat was the laboratory. The rest of the world was where the results were measured. And the results were extraordinary. They are still being measured.

Shiro Saigo
One of the Four Kings · Greatest Fighter of the Early Kodokan · Fifth Dan at Twenty One

A pocket Hercules of a man, well under five feet tall, who at fifty years old came across a group of foreign sailors beating a rickshaw driver on a bridge in Nagasaki. He was drunker than usual. The sailors laughed when they saw him coming: "Scram, midget." In a flash, the pocket Hercules hurled each of them into the river and walked home. He had been throwing larger men since he was sixteen and he never once stopped. His life became the basis of Akira Kurosawa's debut film and then six more films and five television series after that. Saigo became more legend than man, which is what the best students of the best teachers tend to become.

Kyuzo Mifune
Ninth Dan · The God of Judo · Never Defeated in Formal Competition

Grateful to his parents for giving him a small body because it forced him to train twice as hard as everyone else. "If my opponents train one hour I will train two. If they train two I will train three." He was never defeated in formal competition. When he was hospitalised with a life threatening infection Kano sent him a telegram that read simply: "Mifune, don't get thrown." Mifune said that simple message raised his spirit immensely and he recovered. He was still on the mat at seventy three. His five principal points end here: do not place hope in finding a secret technique. Polish the mind through ceaseless training. That is the key.

Yoshitsugu Yamashita
Seventh Dan · Carried Judo to the United States · Taught President Theodore Roosevelt

The philosopher and aesthete among the Four Kings. He carried the Kodokan to the United States in 1902 and eventually gave private lessons to President Roosevelt at the White House. Roosevelt trained with such enthusiasm that he achieved the rank of brown belt, an achievement he considered among the most satisfying of his presidency. This is what judo does to people who approach it seriously and honestly. It reveals something in them that other activities do not reach and cannot find.

Mitsuyo Maeda
Fourth Dan · Founded Brazilian Jiu Jitsu · The Kodokan's Traveling Ambassador

Maeda traveled to the Americas winning dozens of challenge matches across North and South America. In Brazil he taught the Gracie family. What he taught them became, over the following century, Brazilian jiu jitsu, practiced by millions of people worldwide. Every BJJ practitioner alive is in an unbroken lineage a student of the Kodokan. Every submission grappling gym on earth carries a piece of the twelve mat room at Eisho ji Temple. One man, one system, rippling outward still.

Katsuko Kosaki
First Woman Black Belt in the History of the Kodokan · 1933

She found a book about judo in a bookstore when she was eighteen. She read it cover to cover. She decided then and there that judo was for her. She trained in secret from her parents. She defeated three male opponents in a row at the rank examination. In 1933 she became the first woman black belt in the history of the Kodokan. She opened her own dojo two years after that. She was a woman who found a book in a bookstore and decided to become someone different. And she did. Her story became Lady Sanshiro on television. The mat is the answer to the question you are quietly asking about your child right now.

Noriko Yasuda and Keiko Fukuda
Two Women Who Proved What Judo Actually Is

Yasuda came to the Kodokan in 1884, weak and sickly, mentally as well as physically. A year into training she climbed Mount Fuji with a mixed group and reached the summit first. Fukuda was under five feet tall and weighed less than a hundred pounds. She taught judo in the United States into her nineties, through Parkinson's disease and arthritis and two heart attacks and a triple bypass. Her motto: "Be gentle, kind, and beautiful, yet firm and strong, both mentally and physically." She was still on the mat at this writing. The mat is the answer to nearly every objection you have ever raised about whether your child is capable of this.

VII
The Legacy · 1882 to the Present Day

What Survives
a Great Teacher.

Jigoro Kano died in 1938 aboard a ship returning from Cairo where he had attended a meeting of the International Olympic Committee. He was seventy seven years old. He had spent his entire adult life doing two things simultaneously: building the Kodokan into an international institution and campaigning for the inclusion of judo in the Olympic Games. He did not live to see the second goal achieved. Judo became an Olympic sport in 1964 in Tokyo, twenty six years after his death. He had known it would happen. He had set it in motion and trusted that the drops of water would eventually wear down the stone.

His guiding calligraphic motto, the one he returned to most often, brushed at ceremonies and on pieces of paper given away to whoever asked: Hyakuda Fubatsu. One hundred blows, not a single retreat. Stevens writes that this was the guiding principle of Kano's life. It is also the most honest description of what judo training is at every level from white belt to black. Not grace, necessarily. Not elegance, at the beginning. One hundred attempts at the same throw and you do not retreat from the discomfort and eventually the body understands what the mind has been trying to tell it all along.

What Kano left behind is difficult to enumerate and impossible to fully measure. The most widely distributed martial art on earth, practiced in a hundred and forty three countries. The Kodokan, still operational in Tokyo, still training. The concept of systematic physical education which he introduced to Japan and which has since spread through every modern school system on earth. Brazilian jiu jitsu, through Maeda and the Gracies, practiced by millions. Two prime ministers of Japan. A president's judo coach. A generation of Chinese revolutionaries. A famous novelist. A media mogul. And a woman who found the whole thing in a bookstore at eighteen and became the first female black belt in the history of the art.

But the most durable thing Kano left is harder to point to than any of those. It is the idea that physical practice and moral development are not separate activities, that what the body learns the person becomes, and that a discipline designed to train the body in maximum efficiency and mutual benefit will, over time and through enough honest repetition, produce a person who acts with maximum efficiency and mutual benefit in every domain they inhabit. This idea has not aged. It has not been superseded. What sports science now studies Kano already described in 1882 in a twelve mat room at a Buddhist temple with nine students and a purpose.

Kano's calligraphic motto · Brushed many times · Usually without his seal because he could never find it

"Nothing under heaven is more important than education. The teaching of one virtuous person can influence many. That which has been learned well by one generation will pass on to a hundred."

水滴石穿
Drops of Water Wear Down a Stone

Not force. Persistence. The patient application of small and consistent effort over a timescale most people do not have the courage to commit to. This is also a description of how a child develops.

百打不抜
One Hundred Blows, Not a Single Retreat

The guiding principle of Kano's life and the most honest description of what judo training actually is at every level from the first white belt to the last black one.

少壮不努力
No Effort in Youth, No Gain When Old

Not an admonishment. A description of how the body actually works. The window for laying the physical foundation is not as long as we like to assume and it begins earlier than we think.

勤是楽
Movement Is Bliss

Not a consolation. A statement of fact about the physiology of a body that moves regularly and is trained well and is used honestly every day of the life it is given.

盡己竢成
Great Effort, Great Result

"The true road to success is effort, not natural talent or good luck." This is what Stevens calls the summary of Kano's entire philosophy of achievement. It fits on four characters.

明鏡止水
Clear Mirror, Still Water

The ideal state of mind. Polished and serene, like a clear mirror and still water. This is what decades of serious and honest practice can eventually produce in a person who does not retreat.

The club is building toward a founding adult membership of thirty committed practitioners training three sessions per week. This is not an arbitrary number. It is the threshold at which a judo club develops a genuine culture, enough variation in experience and temperament to make every session instructive, and small enough that every member knows every other member by name and by grip and by the way they move under pressure.

Children's enrolment is managed to preserve the quality of individual attention that a premium programme requires. As the club grows toward its after school integration model in 2026, places will be offered to families within the existing membership first, before any external announcement is made.

Membership rates are shared upon direct enquiry. We do not list them publicly because we want the first conversation to be about what the club can do for your family, not about a number on a page that tells you nothing about what you are actually joining.

Sizing Guide · Fighting Films Official

Find your
perfect fit.

The Golden Rule

Always choose a judogi 5cm below your actual height. If you are 170cm tall you select the 165cm judogi. This accounts for the natural stretch of the fabric and ensures the correct competition fit after the first wash. Fighting Films confirm from extensive experience across their entire range that this is the correct method and it is the one they recommend without exception.

Shrinkage Allowance

All Fighting Films judogi have approximately 2% shrinkage on the first cold wash. Wash at 30 degrees or below, air dry naturally and do not use a tumble dryer. Following these care instructions will maintain the judogi's shape and weight and IJF compliance for years of serious competitive use.

Care Instructions

Protect your
investment.

🌡
Cold Wash Only

30 degrees maximum. Higher temperatures degrade the cotton polyester blend, affect the weave density, and can cause shrinkage beyond the expected 2%. IJF compliance over the long term depends on maintaining fabric integrity from the very first wash.

🌬
Air Dry Naturally

Never tumble dry. Hang or lay flat in a well ventilated area and let it dry naturally. Tumble drying causes uneven shrinkage, weakens seams, and reduces collar firmness, and the collar is structurally critical in competition.

📐
Store Flat or Folded

Store flat or neatly folded to maintain collar shape. The collar is the most technically important component of the judogi and it must remain firm enough to be gripped reliably under full competition intensity.

📋
Check Before Competition

Verify skirt length and sleeve measurements before every IJF event. Judogi control is strict and unforgiving. Shrinkage and wear accumulate over time even in the best maintained judogi and must be monitored regularly.
